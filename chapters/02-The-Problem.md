Information, Attention, and the Limits of Professional Capacity

The previous chapter concluded with a question.

If professional judgment is one of an educator’s greatest assets, how might educational systems better support its stewardship throughout a career?

Before that question can be answered, another must first be asked.

Why have we become so effective at storing information, yet so ineffective at carrying forward the understanding that gives that information professional meaning?

At first glance, this question seems almost paradoxical.

Never before have educators possessed greater access to information. Course materials can be preserved indefinitely. Learning management systems retain years of assignments and assessments. Cloud services synchronize documents across devices. Email archives conversations. Institutional systems record enrollment, scheduling, accreditation, assessment, and countless other dimensions of educational work. Every semester leaves behind an increasingly detailed digital record.

By many measures, education has become remarkably successful at preserving information.

Yet many educators experience a different reality.

The beginning of each academic year often feels less like a continuation than a reconstruction. Materials are located rather than remembered. Decisions are revisited because their original reasoning has faded from view. Successful practices are rediscovered almost by accident. Professional experience accumulates, yet the pathways that connect past experience to present judgment gradually weaken.

This is not because educators have forgotten how to teach.

Nor is it because institutions lack information.

The difficulty lies elsewhere.

Educational systems have become increasingly effective at storing information while becoming surprisingly ineffective at preserving the context that gives that information professional meaning.

Information can be archived.

Context must be continually cultivated.

A syllabus records what was taught, but not why it evolved. Meeting minutes preserve decisions, but rarely the conversations that shaped them. Assessment reports capture outcomes without necessarily preserving the professional reflection that gave those outcomes meaning. Files survive. Experience survives. What slowly erodes are the connections between them.

As these connections weaken, educators increasingly become responsible for reconstructing them through their own effort.

Each search through old emails, each attempt to remember why a curriculum changed, each committee that unknowingly revisits a previously resolved question, each new instructor who recreates materials that once existed—these are not isolated inconveniences. They are small acts of reconstruction that collectively consume one of the profession’s most limited resources.

Attention.

Herbert Simon observed that “a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention.” For educators, this observation has become increasingly significant. The challenge is no longer gaining access to information. The challenge is preserving sufficient professional attention to transform that information into thoughtful educational judgment.

Professional attention is inseparable from professional capacity.

Professional judgment concerns the quality of an educator’s decisions. Professional capacity concerns the finite cognitive, emotional, and temporal resources available to make those decisions well.

Unlike digital storage, professional capacity does not expand indefinitely.

Every educator possesses a limited capacity to attend, reflect, remember, mentor, create, communicate, and decide. These limits are not deficiencies to overcome but characteristics of our shared humanity. Educators are not simply professionals. They are parents, partners, caregivers, scholars, artists, neighbors, and members of communities whose responsibilities extend well beyond their institutions. Teaching may be a vocation, but it exists within the larger context of a human life.

The professional role of an educator has expanded dramatically over the past several decades, while the cognitive limits of individual educators have not.

New expectations for assessment, accessibility, accreditation, technology integration, student support, communication, compliance, and institutional accountability have been added to a profession whose central purpose has remained unchanged. In many institutions these expanding responsibilities have coincided with constrained resources, changing faculty composition, increased reliance on contingent appointments, and growing administrative complexity. Regardless of their specific causes, these changes have produced a common reality: educators are increasingly asked to accomplish more while relying on the same finite capacity for attention, reflection, and professional judgment.

None of these responsibilities are inherently misplaced. Most emerged in response to legitimate educational needs and reflect meaningful commitments to students, institutions, and society.

The problem is not that education asks too much of educators because any one responsibility is unreasonable.

The problem is that responsibilities accumulate while professional capacity does not.

Every additional system, requirement, committee, communication channel, assessment process, or reporting expectation competes for the same finite reservoir of human attention.

Simone Weil wrote that attention is “the rarest and purest form of generosity.”

If this is true, then every educational system inevitably participates in a moral decision.

It determines where that generosity will be spent.

The question before us, then, is not whether educators possess enough information.

It is whether the environments in which they work allow their finite professional capacity to remain directed toward the students, colleagues, scholarship, and communities that give education its enduring purpose.

Understanding why that capacity has become increasingly fragmented is the problem to which this chapter now turns.
